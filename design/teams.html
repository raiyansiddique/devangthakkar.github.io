Devang Thakkar

More than the games themselves, what really piqued my interest in American sports is the diversity and creativity you see in the names of the teams. Besides allusions to local connections, these names are also hotbeds for bestial invocations. The use of animals as a sporting demonym is a rather reasonable choice - beastly belligerence is exactly what is required of the cheering audiences (how the El Paso chihuahuas ever made the cut is still a mystery to me though). As a biologist, the team names abstracted themselves in my head to merely different species of animals. This dissociation opens up a whole area of analysis. Are all animals equal or are some animals more equal than others? Are there groups of animals that are over-represented in these team names? Do certain sports prefer certain animals? Before we dive in to these questions, let us first look at the broad statistics of animal representation in team names.

For my analyses, I consider the teams in the professional sports leagues in the US and Canada in seven sports - baseball, basketball, football, hockey, soccer, rugby, and lacrosse. In all, we have with us a total of 703 teams across 56 leagues in 7 sports to begin with. Check out the (add link) Methods section below for a more detailed description of the creation process.

Of 703 teams, we have 175 teams that have an animal or animal-related term in their name. The latter includes teams that have obvious references to animals in their names but not an actual animal. An example would be the Minor League Baseball team Biloxi Shuckers - a shucker is a person who, well, shucks oysters. If you still don't think this should make the cut, check out their logo. (Spoiler alert - IT'S AN OYSTER!)

Figure 1: The names of the beast. All the professional teams across all 7 sports considered for the analysis. The teams with animal references are denoted with white. You may find the raw dataset (link) here.

Going a step further, we can take a look at the split within each sport. Baseball leads the pack with not only the most number of animal names (86) but also the highest ratio of animal names to all team names (86/240 or 35.8%). The other end of the spectrum is led by lacrosse and soccer with 7 animal names each. Not only does soccer have fewest animal teams, it has the lowest ratio across sports, with the animal teams making up only 7.1% (7/99) of all teams. Unsurprisingly, the NFL has the highest number of animal names (15) and contributes to more than half the football tally (15/28) by itself!

Figure 2: The wildest sport of them all. A sport-wise clustering of the teams. The teams with animal references are denoted with white.

The data includes women's professional teams as well, albeit not as many as men's teams. The women in this analysis are represented by the Women's National Basketball Association (WNBA) in Basketball, the National Women's Hockey League (NWHL) and the Canadian Women's Hockey League (CWHL) in Hockey, the National Women's Soccer League (NWSL) in Soccer, and the Women's Professional Lacrosse League (WPSL) and the United Women's Lacrosse League (UWLX) in Lacrosse. The women's teams have much fewer animal names as compared to the men's teams. Out of 41 women's teams, only 3 make the cut - Minnesota Lynx (WNBA), Connecticut Whale (NWHL), and Shenzhen KRS Vanke Rays (CWHL).

Figure 3: Beastly men and civil women. A gendered analysis of the animal team names. The teams with animal references are denoted with white.

These numbers provide a rather clear picture of the overall statistics. What is more interesting to me, however, is the biological aspect of the data. It is said that a picture is worth thousand words, so I decided to visualize the biological hierarchies of the animals in the dataset. For the unitiated, let us first revisit some high school biology - taxonomies, cladograms and the whole shebang.

Humans have been trying to classify organisms since time immemorial - the Egyptians and the Greeks both had systems to differentiate between species. However, it all really began back in the 1700s when an unassuming Swede named Carl Linnaeus published his magna opera Systema Naturae and Species Plantarum. These texts have been considered the foundation of modern taxonomy as we know it today. Linnaeus introduced a binomial nomenclature system wherein each organism is referred to by a two-part name, commonly using Latin grammatical roots. The modern nomeclature system has since been further expanded to classify all living organisms in a systematic manner.

The basic scheme underlying the modern nomenclature system is as follows: Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species. Most organisms that we encounter on a daily basis fall under kingdom Animalia or Plantae - our team animals belong exclusively to kingdom Animalia. One of the most succint ways to represent diverse species in relation to each other is a phylogenetic tree or a cladogram. These two terms refer to essentially the same thing, except that the phylogenetic tree has more quantitative information while the cladogram is more qualitative. For our data, we'll be constructing cladograms that help us visualize the different team animals and how closely they are related to each other.
